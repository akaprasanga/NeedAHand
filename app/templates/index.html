    Journey

    The Process

    Our product uses an object detection model called YOLO. YOLO utilizes a system of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), which is a deep learning algorithm that is used in image recognition and processing and is specifically designed to process pixel data. With YOLO and tedious training, we were able to detect various hand gestures (Right, Left, Up, Stop, and Attack) into classes. With YOLO's live image detection, we can connect our model to a live-footage source, such as a webcam, and use the output from terminal as input for game controls.

    We used 3 sources of data collection:

    1. Web-scraping: the use of images from Google
    2. Public databases
    3. Webcam footage

    We collected and organized all our data on a website called Roboflow.While collecting data, we divided all the different gestures (left, right, up, stop, and attack) across the 5 of us. We collected and annotated our individual gesture. After our first training session, we realized we needed more data, so we individually took webcam footage for all the other gestures we didn't do to better the results. During our pre-processing, we split the images with 70% for training (647 image), 20% for validation (183 images), and 10% for testing (97 images). This totaled around 930 images. We then resized all the images to 416 x 416 pixels and unified the class names to "Left", "Right", "Up", "Stop", and "Attack". We finally augmented our data using grayscale, hue, saturation, brightness, exposure, blur, and noise. This in turn tripled our dataset, making our model more robust.

    For training our model, we used CoCalc's virtual machine software and ran our dataset through it. We used the Yolov5s model (2nd smallest YOLO model) in order to achieve decent frame rate object detection on live-footage with the sacrifice of the model being slightly inaccurate. Our first training session had many flaws, namely the model falsely detecting hand gestures in the background of images or mixing up gestures with one another, or detecting hand gestures at all. On our final data collection and training session, we removed all the clip-art gathered from the first round of web-scrapping and added more images of each gesture with various positions to increase variability, allowing our model to achieve viably accurate detections.

    After finalizing our model, we moved onto visibly presenting our model. We created a website using the assistance of a Bootstrap template and incorporated our model by allowing users to upload images of certain hand gestures, resulting in the identification of the gesture as well as the confidence in the identification. We also added our game into the website, where users can use their webcam to control the character straight from the website itself. This process came with many minor bugs that took a tedious amount of time to figure out and solve, but it successfully resulted in a website that we are all proud of.

    We faced many roadblocks throughout this adventure of the 3-week program, but through the entire group's dedication and especially our Professor's mentorship, we managed to overcome all of them. Our main roadblocks includes:

    1. The Left and Right Gestures overlapped and our model would be frequently confused by the two gestures.
    2. There were multiple gestures being detected at once and gestures being detected in the background.
    3. The trade-off we had to accept with the usage of a smaller YOLO model.

    In order to fix these problems, we added a substantial amount of left and right hand gestures. There was also a built in hyper-parameter in our mode that automatically flipped our images 50% of the time called "fliplr". We disabled this parameter and solved the issue. In order to stop the model from falsely detecting background objects, we increased the model's confidence threshold.

    Motives

    What motivated us to work on this project

    We wanted to see if we could find a way to combine both game development and the lessons of AI Camp.

    We wanted to do something with hand-tracking.

    We figured that this project would be really beneficial for us as it was a challenging one, meaning that we would learn a lot of stuff as we worked on it.

    We knew we would face a bunch of roadblocks such as the time constraint and finding a way to connect the hand gesture controls to a game.
